Introduction To Bitcoin

What is Bitcoin?

In short, Bitcoin is a electronic payment system based on mathematical proof.

The digital currency is decentralized and independent of any central authority or bank. Using peer-to-peer technology with transactions and the issuing of Bitcoin to be carried out collectively by the network. Balances are stored on a public ledger in the cloud and Bitcoin transactions are verified by computing power in the form of miners.

Proposed in 2008 by the mysterious Satoshi Nakamoto, who's identity has still yet to be discovered. The white paper outlined a borderless payment system which promised low fees, fast transactions and the freedom of money.

Bitcoin differs from fiat money in several important ways:

1. Decentralised
Bitcoin is a distributed and open network, owned by nobody. This makes the digital currency decentralized and means no single authority, bank or institution controls the network. Bitcoin is maintained by volunteer coders and run by an open network of dedicated computers spread around the world. Giving individuals and groups back control over their money

2. Fixed Supply
Based on the concept of gold being mined. Bitcoin also has a maximum limit of 21 million which can be in existence. This is to counter the manipulation and inflammatory nature of fiat currency. The idea is that over time, bitcoins mined will decrease and thus become scarcer over time. In theory this will intern increase the demand.

3. Anonymity
Unlike traditional payments, in theory. Bitcoin users are semi-anonymous, as there is no central validator requiring users to identify themselves when making a transaction. In practise to stop criminals, terrorists and money-launderers most exchanges are required by law to perform identity checks on customers. Transactions can be tracked with wallet addresses and linked to users in most cases.

4. Fixed Transactions
Unlike electronic fiat transactions, Bitcoin cannot be reversed. As a transaction is recorded on the network, and if more than an hour passes, it is impossible to modify as there is no central body. Whilst this may discourage some, it does make sure transactions cannot be tampered with.

5. Divisibility
Due to Bitcoin be based on mathematical protocols, making it possible to complete micro transactions. Not possible in current electronic money. The smallest unit is one hundred millionth of a Bitcoin (0.00000001) and called a satoshi.

How to store your Bitcoin

Once you have purchased your Bitcoin, you will want the digital asset to be stored in a safe place. In this section we will provide a description of cryptocurrency wallets and information on different wallets that can be used.

In simple terms a cryptocurrency wallet allows users to store and retrieve their digital assets.

From a technical standpoint Bitcoin is digital and not stored in a physical place like cash or gold. However the aspects which are stored are the
public addresses and secret digital keys. A cryptocurrency wallet uses a software program that stores public address and private keys. Which
interacts with various blockchain to enable users to access and make transactions with their digital currency.

From a user perspective a cryptocurrency wallet is equivalent to a physical wallet and you will be able to make transactions and monitor your
balance. It is important to know the different types of wallets in order to keep your Bitcoin and other digital assets safe. These can be found
below:

Web
A wallet stored on a server, which is constantly online and controlled by a third party. They are often linked to mobile apps. When purchasing
Bitcoin through Binance, Coinbase etc, the stored currency is in an online wallet.

The method means users have easy access to their funds however it is controlled by a third party. It is important to make sure the exchange or
other web wallet provider is safe and trustworthy. There has been instances in the past of exchanges shutting down and users loosing their
funds. Our recommended list of exchanges can be found here.

Desktop Wallet
A wallet that can be downloaded and stored on your computer hard drive. They are still connected to the internet however they don’t rely on a
third party.

This makes them still at risk from online hacks however they are more secure as they are controlled by the you, the user.
Hardware Wallet
Hardware wallets are the most secure way of storing you Bitcoins or alternative digital currencies.
Hardware wallets are physical devices that store users public addresses and private keys.

These wallets can be used to securely interact and as they are offline are exempt from hacking. They will often have a pin or access code for
extra security if it is ever lost or stolen.
We have created a list of the most trusted hardware wallets which can be found here.
Mobile
Mobile wallets run as apps on your smartphone, storing your private keys and allowing you to pay for things directly from your phone.

They are a great solution for convenience and easy access however as they are online they can be hacked. as well as your phone at risk of being
misplaced.

Paper
Essentially a document with your public address and private keys written on. It often has a QR code on that can be scanned for quick access and
transactions.

A benefit of this method is the information is stored offline however you are printing valuable information and should make sure it is kept in a
safe place where only you have access.

Physical Bitcoin

It is now possible to have a “physical Bitcoin” (as such). It is a glamorous way of having your public address and public keys written down. There
are different types but they all work similarly with information hidden under a reelable hologram and when removed, it leaves a tamper evident
mark. Once the physical Bitcoin is redeemed it looses its digital worth

They have become more like a collectors item and like paper wallets are offline so makes them immune to online hacks however they need to
be stored in a safe place.
Introduction to Margin Trading

Margin trading accounts allow traders to access greater sums by “borrowing” capital. This enhances a traders
buying power enabling traders to open position far larger than their real account balance. It also allows traders to long and short the market, which means traders can profit in
both a bullish and bearish market.

Margin trading is also referred to as leverage trading. Leverage is the amount by which a trader is able to multiply their position. This usually
ranges between 1X to 100X leverage.

An example of using 100X leverage: If a trader uses a margin of $36 with 100X leverage. On a margin trading platform this enables you trade
$36 X 100, which would give you $3,600 worth of Bitcoin contracts.

Whilst this can be a great method to amplify profits. It is risky and exposes a trader to heavy losses. It is recommended to trade within 0 - 25X
leverage.

Margin trading is possible as the trader puts down an “ initial margin” on a trade and is required to keep a certain amount of capital in their
account, called the “maintenance margin”. If a position is suffering a loss, you might become "liquidated". This where the loss on the position
is approaching or becomes equal to the initial margin you committed to the position. This alongside the maintenance margin ensures your
account does not go into negative funds.

When opening a position on a margin platform you can either go long or short. If you believe the digital asset will rise you will open a long
position. If you wish to profit from the digital asset going down in price , you will open a short position.

Staying Safe

Unfortunately scams are common in the digital spaced it is important to keep your personal details and funds safe. We want to help users
navigate the cryptocurrency space with ease but we want to make sure all users are doing this safely. Below is a few guidelines to raise your
awareness of measures you can take to ensure that you do not fall victim to a scam:

Make sure all websites you visit have a SSL certificate. This means that all passwords and information inputted on the website will be encrypted.
An SSL certificate is a secure padlock in the browser.

Unless you are signed up to a website mailing list or have listed an account with an exchange. It is unlikely that a website, exchange, influencer
etc will reach out to you first. Especially with requests to deposit funds to unsolicited deposit addresses.

Always check rewards or promotions on the companies website. Nobody can offer “guaranteed profits”.

Always check the email domain. For instance BitcoinNav email will end in “@bitcoinnav.com”.

Never reveal your public keys to anybody. If you are to transfer digital coins to an address, this does not require you to release your private key
information. You will only need the public address of the intended recipient of the digital coins.

We highly recommend setting up 2 Factor Authentication (2FA) when using exchanges. 2FA is a method in which you are only able to gain
access after successful inputting the generated numbers. Often used with SMS, email or google authenticator. This further protects your
accounts.

Finally, if something seems to good to be true. Chances are it is.
